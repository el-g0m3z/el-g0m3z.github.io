What is Stoicism?

Stoicism is a philosophy that teaches the development of self-control and fortitude as a means of overcoming destructive emotions. The philosophy holds that becoming a clear and unbiased thinker allows one to understand the universal reason (logos).

Who were the Stoics?

The three most famous Stoic philosophers are Seneca, Epictetus, and Marcus Aurelius.

  • Seneca was a Roman statesman and philosopher who lived from 4 BCE to 65 CE. He is known for his writings on ethics, including his letters to Lucilius, which offer practical advice on how to live a good life.
  • Epictetus was a Greek Stoic philosopher who lived from 55 to 135 CE. He was born a slave but later gained his freedom and became a prominent teacher of Stoicism. His teachings emphasize the importance of personal responsibility and the idea that we cannot control external events, only our responses to them.
  • Marcus Aurelius was a Roman emperor who lived from 121 to 180 CE. He is known for his philosophical writings, particularly his book "Meditations," which is a collection of his personal reflections on Stoic philosophy and how to apply it to daily life.

Teachings and Phrases

Some key teachings and phrases from Stoicism include:

Memento Mori - Memento Mori is a Latin phrase that translates to "Remember that you will die." It serves as a reminder of the inevitability of death and encourages individuals to live a meaningful and virtuous life, focusing on what truly matters.

Memento Vivere - Memento Vivere is a Latin phrase that translates to "Remember to live." It emphasizes the importance of living life to the fullest and not getting caught up in trivial matters.

Amathia - A greek word that roughly translates to Intelligent Stupidity. It explains why otherwise smart people do evil and stupid things. It is not that people lack the ability of understanding (normal stupidity) but are unwilling to learn (thus intelligent stupidity). It is not anger that is manly, but gentleness and delicacy. It is because they are more human that they are more manly; they possess more strength, more nerve, and more virility” It is better to be the person wronged than the one who is wronging others. true happiness only results from treating other people with fairness and kindness.

Eudaimonia - Sometimes anglicized as eudaemonia or eudemonia, is a Greek word literally translating to the state or condition of 'good spirit', and which is commonly translated as 'happiness' or 'welfare'. In the works of Aristotle, eudaimonia was the term for the highest human good in older Greek tradition.
